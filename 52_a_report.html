5.2. A report

Model text

EXCITEMENT AT THE SEVILLE MARATHON

Diego Máquez. Seville, 14 March 2025

Runners reaching the finish line.

Seville, March 2, 2025 – Thousands of runners filled the streets yesterday for the annual Seville Marathon, enjoying perfect weather and cheering crowds.

The race began at 8 a.m. near Plaza de España. Among the participants was 17-year-old Marta López, running her first marathon. “I trained for months, and I’m excited,” she said.

Runners passed iconic landmarks like the Cathedral and the Guadalquivir River. Many struggled near the 35-kilometer mark, but the crowd’s energy kept them going. David Fernández, 22, shared, “I was exhausted, but the support helped me finish.”

Kenyan runner Joseph Kiplagat won in 2 hours and 10 minutes. Local athlete Antonio Muñoz, finishing in 2 hours and 35 minutes, said, “Running in my city is a dream.”

The event ended with hugs, photos, and celebrations, proving once again that the Seville Marathon is more than just a race—it’s a test of endurance and community spirit.

A report structure

A report presents a more detailed account of an event that is of interest to readers. It can offer a broader view of a news story, although reports can also be written about topics that are not current. Some tips to write a report:

A report has the following parts:

  • Headline – It is the title of the news report. It is short and catches the reader’s attention.

    Example: "Thousands Run in Seville Marathon"

  • Author, date and time.

  • Lead – It is the first sentence or paragraph. It gives the most important information: Who? What? When? Where?

    Example: "Seville, March 2, 2025 – Thousands of runners filled the streets yesterday for the annual Seville Marathon."

  • Image and caption (description of the image)

  • Body – It gives more details about the event. It answers Why? How? and includes quotes, facts, and descriptions.

    Example: "The race began at 8 a.m. near Plaza de España. Among the participants was 17-year-old Marta López, running her first marathon."

  • Closing – It is the last part. It gives a final thought or a conclusion.

    Example: "The event ended with hugs, photos, and celebrations, proving once again that the Seville Marathon is more than just a race—it’s a test of endurance and community spirit."

Useful language

1. Headline (Title)
"Big Crowd at [Event]"
"[Person] Wins [Competition]"
"[Event] Happened in [Place]"


2. Lead (Introduction: Who? What? When? Where?)
"[Event] took place in [Place] on [Date]."
"[Number] people joined [Event] yesterday."
"[Person] said, 'I am happy to be here!'"

3. Body (Details: Why? How? Quotes?)
"Many people enjoyed the event because..."
"[Person] explained, 'It was very exciting.'"
"The crowd cheered as [Person] finished the race."

4. Closing (Final Thought, Impact)
"Everyone had a great time at [Event]."
"[Event] was a big success for the city."
"People are already looking forward to next year."

General tips

  • Use a clear and catchy headline.
  • Dive deeper into the events. 
  • Start with the most important information (Lead). Answer Who? What? When? Where? in the first sentence. Keep it short and clear.
  • Give more details in the body. Answer Why? How? and include facts.
  • Write in a neutral and objective tone. A news report gives facts, not opinions.
  • Use short paragraphs and simple sentences.
  • End with a strong conclusion. Summarize the event. Mention its importance or future impact.
  • Check grammar and spelling.
